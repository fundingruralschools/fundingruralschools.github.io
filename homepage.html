What is the Secure Rural Schools Program?

Following the creation of the United States Forest Service in 1905, the Act of May 23, 1908--today, known as the Secure Rural Schools and Community Self Determination Act was created. This act works to help compensate counties with growing amounts of forest service land for the potential tax money which they were losing by not building on the acres of protected land. Payments to counties are broken into three categories: Title I for roads and schools, Title II for projects on federal lands, and Title III for county projects.


To learn more about the origins of the Secure Rural Schools Program, click here to visit the National Forest Service website.

Alpine County

Alpine County received the largest per capita amount of school funding in California, totaling $454,076 ($403 per capita). The median household income is $119,659, which is relatively high, but 5.5% of residents live in poverty, indicating significant economic challenges. The county also has a much lower percentage of residents with a bachelor's degree or higher compared to Sierra and Trinity.

Sierra County

Sierra County received $866,529 ($272 per capita). The median household income is $73,529, significantly lower than median household income in California, $96,334. Trinity County

Trinity County received $3,545,813 ($226 per capita). Trinity County also has the highest poverty rate among the three counties, with more than 12% of families below the poverty line. It also has the highest percentage of residents with a bachelor's degree or higher, suggesting an educated population despite economic hardships. The median family income is lower than Alpine's, reinforcing the financial struggles that affect local schools.
